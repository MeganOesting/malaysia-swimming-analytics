MOT Delta Analysis: Data Methodology & Assumptions

This report explains the statistical foundation and methodological assumptions underlying the Malaysia On Track (MOT) Delta Analysis Project. Understanding these principles is essential for interpreting the analysis results correctly.

1. Data Source Overview

Our analysis is based on USA Swimming season rankings from the 2021-2022, 2022-2023, 2023-2024, and 2024-2025 seasons. For each event, gender, and age combination, we have access to the top 500 ranked swimmers from the USA Swimming database.

Important: USA Swimming seasons run from September 1 of the first year to August 31 of the second year. Ages are based on the swimmer's age on the first day of competition, not their birth year age.

2. Distribution Shape: Why It's Not Normal

2.1 The Full Population Distribution

If we could see all swimmers (not just competitive ones), the distribution of swim times would be:

  • Highly right-skewed: A long tail of slower times, with most swimmers being slower and fewer being very fast.
  • Not normal: The classic "bell curve" would not apply. Instead, we'd see a distribution where the mean is much greater than the median, pulled rightward by many slower swimmers.
  • Truncated by participation: The full population excludes:
    • People who don't swim
    • People who swim but don't compete
    • Competitors who don't achieve official times

2.2 Our Elite Sample (Top 500)

What we actually have is a left-truncated elite sample. We only see the fastest 500 swimmers in each category, representing the right tail (elite portion) of what would be a much larger distribution. The distribution within our sample may appear somewhat normal or less skewed, but this is because we're only looking at a narrow, elite slice. We cannot extrapolate our statistics to the full population of swimmers.

2.3 Why This Matters

Understanding the truncated nature of our data is crucial because:

  • Z-scores are relative: A z-score in our dataset compares a swimmer to other elite swimmers, not to all swimmers
  • Percentiles are meaningful: "Top 10%" in our dataset means top 10% of elite competitive swimmers, which is genuinely exceptional
  • Our "average" is elite: The mean and median in our sample represent elite performance, not typical recreational swimming

3.2 What This Means

We are capturing the elite portion of competitive swimmers. For popular events like 50 Free, our top 500 represents approximately the top 7-12% of competitive swimmers in that age group. This means:

  • The slowest swimmer in our dataset (#500) is already in the top ~10% of all competitive swimmers
  • Even "below average" swimmers in our sample are still elite compared to the full population
  • Our standards are appropriately set for identifying and tracking elite developmental swimmers

4. Why This Approach Is Appropriate for MOT

The truncated, elite nature of our data is not a limitation—it's a feature for the MOT project:

  1. Elite Standards: MOT is designed to identify swimmers on track for elite performance. Setting standards based on the top 500 (elite competitive swimmers) is exactly what we want.
  2. Developmental Tracking: By focusing on elite performance patterns, we can identify which improvement trajectories lead to success at higher levels.
  3. International Benchmarking: Comparing our elite sample to Canada On Track reference times (which are also elite standards) provides meaningful insights for setting Malaysian elite developmental targets.
  4. Statistical Validity: Within our elite sample, statistical measures (z-scores, percentiles, improvement rates) are valid and meaningful. We're comparing "elite to elite," which is appropriate for our goals.

Key Insight: We don't need to include slower swimmers in our analysis. The top 500 elite swimmers provide sufficient data to understand improvement patterns and set appropriate developmental standards. Including recreational or less-competitive swimmers would actually dilute the precision of our elite-focused analysis.

5. Limitations and Assumptions

5.1 Explicit Assumptions

  • Elite Focus: We assume that MOT standards should be based on elite competitive performance, not average or recreational swimming.
  • USA Swimming as Proxy: We use USA Swimming data as a proxy for elite developmental patterns, recognizing that cultural, training, and competitive environments may differ in Malaysia.
  • Top 500 as Representative: We assume the top 500 swimmers capture a sufficient sample of elite performance to identify meaningful patterns and trends.
  • Age-Based Analysis: We assume that improvement patterns between ages 15→16, 16→17, and 17→18 are consistent enough to provide meaningful guidance for developmental tracking.

5.2 Limitations

  • Cannot Extrapolate: Our statistics cannot be extrapolated to the full population of swimmers. Z-scores and percentiles are only valid within our elite sample.
  • Truncation Effects: We cannot observe swimmers outside the top 500, so we cannot analyze the full distribution shape or understand what happens below the elite threshold.
  • Cultural Differences: USA Swimming data may not perfectly reflect Malaysian swimming development patterns, though it provides the best available proxy for elite developmental trajectories.
  • Temporal Stability: We assume that improvement patterns observed across 2021-2025 are relatively stable and will remain relevant for future MOT applications.

6. Implications for MOT Standards

Understanding our data's characteristics directly informs how MOT standards should be interpreted:

  • Elite Benchmarking: MOT times are set relative to elite competitive swimmers, making them appropriately challenging for identifying developmental talent.
  • Z-Score Interpretation: A z-score of 1.5 in our dataset means a swimmer is in the top ~6.7% of elite competitive swimmers, which translates to exceptional performance even within this elite group. See our comprehensive Z-Score Analysis Report for detailed analysis of how improvement rates vary by performance level (z-score) across all transitions.
  • Improvement Expectations: Improvement deltas (15→16, 16→17, 17→18) reflect patterns observed in elite swimmers, providing realistic benchmarks for swimmers on track for elite performance.
  • Track Identification: The correlation between performance level (z-score) and improvement rate helps identify which swimmers are "on track" for elite development, even if their current times are slower than some peers.

7. Data Quality and Validation

All data has been validated to ensure:

  • Time Parsing: Swim times are correctly parsed from various formats (MM:SS.ss, SS.ss, etc.)
  • Range Validation: Times are validated against realistic bounds for each event (e.g., 400 Free: 230-600 seconds, accommodating elite times down to 3:50)
  • Completeness: Files are checked for expected 500 results per event/gender/age combination
  • Consistency: Event names, gender codes, and age categories are standardized across all analyses

Any flagged records are manually verified to distinguish between legitimate elite times and data errors.
