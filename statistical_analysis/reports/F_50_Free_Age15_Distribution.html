üìä What is a Z-Score?

Simple Explanation: A z-score tells you how good a swimmer's time is compared to everyone else.

  • Positive z-score (green): Faster than average. Example: +2.0 means much faster than most swimmers.
  • Zero z-score: Exactly average.
  • Negative z-score (red): Slower than average. Example: -1.0 means slower than most swimmers.

Real Example: If a swimmer has a z-score of +1.5, their time is 1.5 standard deviations faster than the average. This is quite good!

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: This is NOT a Complete Bell Curve

This data only includes the top 500 swimmers in the USA. We don't see slower swimmers because:

  • The USA Swimming rankings only show the best 500 times per season
  • Many slower swimmers exist but aren't in this dataset
  • This creates a "cutoff" or "truncated" distribution

What this means: The slowest swimmer in our data (#500) is probably much better than the average 15-year-old female swimmer in the USA. We're only looking at the elite pool!

Graph 2a: Time Density Distribution (Bell Curve Portion)

What this shows: The density (frequency) distribution of swim times - this is the portion of the bell curve we're seeing from the top 500 swimmers. The y-axis shows the density (probability density) at each time value.

Why this matters: This shows the "cutoff" effect - we're only seeing the right tail of what would be a full bell curve. The slowest swimmer (#500) represents a cutoff point - many slower swimmers exist but aren't in our dataset.

Graph 2b: Z-Score Density Distribution (Bell Curve Portion)

What this shows: The density distribution of z-scores - showing the portion of the bell curve we're capturing. The y-axis shows the density at each z-score value.

Why this matters: This visualizes the "truncated" distribution. Notice how the left side (negative z-scores) is cut off - we don't see the full bell curve because we only have the top 500 swimmers. This helps explain why even "elite" swimmers in our dataset can have negative z-scores relative to the mean of these 500.

üîó What Does Correlation Mean?

Correlation measures how two things relate to each other:

  • Positive correlation (+): When one thing goes up, the other also goes up.
    Example: Height and shoe size (taller people usually have bigger feet)
  • Negative correlation (-): When one thing goes up, the other goes down.
    Example: Practice time and race time (more practice = faster time = lower number)
  • No correlation (near 0): The two things don't relate to each other.
    Example: Favorite color and swimming speed

For Our Data: Z-Score vs. Improvement

What we're asking: Do faster swimmers (high z-scores) improve more than slower swimmers?

  • If correlation is +0.35: Faster swimmers tend to improve MORE (positive relationship)
  • If correlation is -0.35: Faster swimmers tend to improve LESS (negative relationship)
  • If correlation is +0.012 (1.2%): Very weak positive relationship - almost no connection

Real-World Meaning: A correlation of 1.2% means there's almost no relationship between how fast a swimmer is at age 15 and how much they'll improve by age 16. Both fast and slow swimmers improve about the same amount!
