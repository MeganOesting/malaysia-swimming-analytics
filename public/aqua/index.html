Understanding AQUA Points

The World Aquatics Points Table (commonly called AQUA Points or FINA Points) enables comparison of results across different swimming events. The system assigns point values to performances, allowing fair comparison between different strokes, distances, and genders.

Key Characteristics:

  • Universal Comparison: Compare performances across different events, strokes, distances, and genders
  • World Record Benchmark: 1000 points equals the current world record pace
  • Annual Updates: Base times are updated annually based on world records
  • Course Specific: Separate tables for Long Course (LCM) and Short Course (SCM)

The Formula

AQUA Points are calculated using a cubic curve formula:

P = 1000 x (B / T)^3
VariableDescriptionExample
PPoints awarded850 points
BBase time (world record, in seconds)46.86s (M 100 Free WR)
TSwimmer's time (in seconds)49.50s

Note: Point values are truncated to whole numbers (not rounded).

Example Calculation: Male 100m Freestyle

Swimmer's time: 49.50 seconds

Base time (WR): 46.86 seconds

Calculation:

  • Ratio: 46.86 / 49.50 = 0.9467
  • Cubed: 0.9467^3 = 0.8485
  • Points: 1000 x 0.8485 = 848 points

Base Times

Base times are established annually for all standard individual events and relays:

Update Schedule:

CourseCutoff DatePublished
Short Course (SCM)August 31stWithin 1 month (by October)
Long Course (LCM)December 31stWithin 1 month (by February)

Base Time Categories:

  • Gender: Separate base times for Men and Women
  • Course: Separate tables for Long Course (50m) and Short Course (25m)
  • Events: All standard individual events and relays

Base times derive from the latest World Aquatics-approved world records. When a world record is broken, the base time is updated in the next annual publication.

Why Cubic (Not Linear)?

The cubic formula (power of 3) creates a non-linear scale that:

  • Rewards Excellence: Small improvements at faster times are worth more points than the same improvement at slower times
  • Reflects Reality: It's exponentially harder to improve as you approach world record pace
  • Fair Comparison: Normalizes the relative difficulty of achieving times across different events
